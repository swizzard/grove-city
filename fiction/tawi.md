He saw Kone and 'Awi's 'Ikett by the stall, chewing his stalk ruminatively. He greeted 'Ikett and Peko the _'i'i_ vendor, and nodded when Peko asked if he wanted his usual. As he waited, Tawi made the customary small talk.
"How are your fowl, 'Ikett?" he asked.
"Mmm." 'Ikett replied gloomily, "Assets in other pursuits, but not in this: I woo Pepa and TTa'o's KKatto." With his proclamation, 'Ikett raised his eyes to meet Tawi's and brandished his half-finished _'i'i_ vibrantly. The speed at which he returned to his previous mien suggested to Tawi that his childhood neighbor had not been particularly successful so far. 
'Ikett had always been kind—he spoiled the _'owi_ they and the others in their _neppomammo_ had to take care of the summer before they came of age, smuggling extra seeds and bits of fritter up to the coop on roof of their building. Kindness alone gets you little, and 'Ikett never seemed to have much more to offer. Though they were both nearly twenty years older, Tawi saw on him the same look as when 'Ommi Mawwe explained what would happen to their _'owi_ when it grew too old to lay eggs and protect the garden from bugs. 
"You have fixed your gaze on a vivid flower," Tawi offered by means of compliment. "I'm sorry it yet remains distant."
"Peko suggested a meal. KKatto and me. Together, I mean."
"Indeed I did suggest a meal," Peko interjected. He had been preparing Tawi's _'i'i_: peeling the end of a stalk and dipping it in syrup. He coated the sticky end first in crushed pepper and then in ground lime leaf and handed it to Tawi, who took it graciously. The younger man held up his hand palm-forward and splayed his fingers in the universal plainsfolk gesture of _I'll pay you back, honest_. Peko sighed. "My _nnonna_ said a meal can move a heart more than a live _kkippi_," he said. "Scores of bachelors buried and reburied the same promises to win his _'ommi_ 'Itaw, but when she saw that his _'ammi_ Mopmo had both roasted her an _'owi_ and besides had made her favorite dish, _ggeni wa kkamonn_, her heart was his forever."
"I don't know how to cook," 'Ikett sighed, "And my roommates would not let me. And she would not feel comfortable in my house. And—"
"What if someone else prepared the meal?" Tawi cut him off.
"Should I present KKatto like a baby bird to be snatched up by some stranger with a _nannka_?"
"No," Tawi tried to calm him down. "No, it is still yours—you get the food, but only someone else chops and stirs. Do you know the plainsfolk wet market on the Street of Animalworkers, by Third Withriver Street?"
"The place that burned down?"
"The _Low_ have built it again," Peko said, "I have not been but my pponni PPiko has."
"Your _pponni_ PPiko and anybody?" Tawi inquired.
Peko glared down at him from inside the booth. "Sniff elsewhere, _'akow_. She fixes her gaze on those who make good on their debts."
"I can afford to be curious, at least," Tawi replied, turning back to 'Ikett. "The _nnotti_ of my _nnonna_ is in charge there. He says his _neponno_ keeps a fire with _nimmko_ and a _nannka_ going in one of the corners of the place all the time. You get them to cook what you get from the market. They give you back cooked what you got at the market."
"Then," 'Ikett perked up somewhat. "I could get KKatto's favorite foods."
"Yes, you could." Tawi nodded. "And even though 'Ato and his _konna_ cook the food, she'll know they are from you."
"Then I pray to the _wwoma_ of my _wwoma_ that KKatto's favorite food is smells," said 'Ikett, deflating again. "I have nothing to spend at the market."
"'Ommi 'Atto says nobody with _nniwak_ has nothing." By the look on 'Ikett's face, his _'ama_'s favorite saying was having its usual limited effect. "And Tawi is almost as good," he added.
'Ikett immediately brightened; Peko snorted derisively. "Is Tawi worth as much as these?" he asked, taking one shallow wooden bowl down from a crowded shelf.
