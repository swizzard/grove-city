# folklore
* valorizes tricksters, livestock thieves, explorers
* no national epic or even collectively-owned narratives
* "great-great-granduncle Pemi," "'Awo, grandma's neighbor's grandfather," etc
    * unclear if they're archetypal folk figures or if there really was a "historical 'cousin Tiwo'"
    * "great grandpa Genghis Khan used to…"
* creator/sky god 'e-Me'iwa
* WWama Mo'anna & WWoma Mitto
    * one day, WWama Mo'anna saw 'i'i growing & goes to get it, leaving WWoma Mitto alone so she cries. WWama comes back & WWoma dips them &c. WWama then leaves to get 'owi, WWoma cries. WWama comes back, WWoma builds 'owi a house out of 'i'i. WWama leaves to get yams, WWoma cries, WWama returns, WWoma makes sweet potato jam. WWama goes to get kkippi, WWoma cries, WWama comes back, kkippi is charmed by smell of sweet potato jam. when they run out of 'i'i, WWoma says, let's go together, so they traveled around, following things as they grew. eventually they came back to where they had been and found wild rice growing where WWoma had cried
    * one day, WWama is bored and wants to go look for something new. WWoma suggests they make new things instead & WWama agrees, as long as it's a contest. WWama takes an 'eke tree & makes 'Ama Ke'a, the first ke-ke'a, while WWoma takes an 'ete bush & makes 'Oma Ke'a, the first te-ke'a. They agree it's a tie—WWama loves how beautiful and helpful 'Oma Ke'a is and WWoma is charmed by 'Ama Ke'a's strength and deliberateness. WWama gives 'Oma Ke'a an invisible magic piti to spy for her and WWoma gives 'Ama Ke'a a spade and a basket and teaches him to also do agriculture
    * 'Ama Ke'a and 'Oma Ke'a have 5 children
        * 'Ammi Wennta
        * 'Ommi 'Atto
        * 'Ommi 'Anno
            * invents cooking & storing food
        * 'Ammi 'Ekinn
        * 'Ammi KKi'e
            * the baby
            * is Baby
        * eldest two (Wennta & 'Atto) are twins
    * WWama & WWoma ask NNotti KKippi to watch them, →NNonna KKippi
        * NNonna KKippi teaches them how to ride, etc
        * PPonni 'Owi helps out
        * they invent wrestling
    * they find a beehive (we-wwitte)
        * Wennta sees the beehive first, gets stung trying to open it
        * 'Atto approaches with burning 'i'i, the smoke of which subdues the hive but 'Atto can't carry anything back
* PPonni 'Owi - Cousin 'Owi
    * 'Akama Ponno 'Owi is scared of everything, WWama rescues them & they ask WWama to take care of them
* NNotti KKippi - Cousin KKippi
    * challenges (is tricked into challenging?) WWama to ride them, they go all the way back to camp where NNoto KKippi smells WWoma's sweet potato jam & decides to stay

##  holidays
* 5 major seasonal festivals
    * 'i'iko me-nikit - 'i'i festival (ítcdzâtʃèè/.60, end of rainy season)
        * babies/birth, flowers/flowering plants, nniwak
        * flower garlands/crowns
        * games
        * footraces
        * traditionally rock-throwing/-slinging but that's not feasible in city but it's become the start of slap ball season
        * new garlands for the altar 
    * 'owiko me-nikit - 'owi festival (dzâtʃádzîk/ , beginning of windy season)
        * toddlers/children, (young) families, home-making
        * celebrated with your family
        * games
            * rugby
            * tag
        * foods
            * wamakako ggeni
                * stash eggs in ashes overnight, eat them for breakfast
            * owiko nimmko
                *  ~terrine
    * a'iko me-nikkit - 'a'i festival (ítcîtcdzìk/ , middle of windy season
        * mothers, older women, patience, innovation, storytelling
        * games
            * 'a'i-growing contests
            * storytelling contests
            * showing off, exchanging crafts
        * foods
            * a'iko 'eto
            * sweets
    * kkippiko me-nikit - kkippi festival (îtcdzàdzìk/ , end of windy season)
        * fathers, older men, diligence, persistence
        * singing, dancing, music, drama, etc
        * games
    * ttintako me-nikit - ttinta festival (ítcîtctʃèè/.19, middle of rainy season)
        * also called wwomako me-nikit
        * the elderly, the passage of time, reflection
        * 'end of the year'
        * visits with/gifts for grandparents/anyone over 65
        * 'a'immiko we-'ettin/'a'immiko we-nnattin - "interring intentions"/"unwrapping intentions" ~ new year's resolutions
            *  traditionally you recorded your goal(s) for the year in a clay jar you literally buried & dug up
            *  reflect on how you did/what you want to do
        * clean up balcony/roof space
        * throw away old garlands & incense
        * ritually cleanse/'reset' altar
        * no games
        * foods
            * ttakita ttinta - puffed rice, in 2 styles
                * mmakka - tossed in honey syrup, formed into shapes (circles, flowers)
                * ppa'a - basically jhalmuri
            * ttinta wa 'owi - ttinta cooked in a nannka with more liquid than most plains folk dishes, almost a stew many babies' first solid food, also fed to sick/elderly
                * comfort food
            * ttintako mmeppo - rice wine
            * po'emmannke ttinta - mochi-like cakes made from mashed overcooked rice
* 40ish days between ttintako & 'i'iko are called me-'i-tto'e me-'i-pew "fallow days" or 'a-WWama nnan me-'i-pew "days without Grandfather" & are a rest period commemorating WWoma Mitto waiting sadly for WWama Mo'anna to come back for the first time. starting something new during this time is considered unlucky, children born during this time are stereotyped as spooky
* ancestors' birthdays
    * exact dates get fuzzy so certain days/times of year are important to certain clans/families
